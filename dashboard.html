📚 Lesson 1

Build your first real budget.

A budget isn't a punishment — it's permission. It's the difference between checking your balance and praying, vs knowing exactly what you have, what's coming, and what you can actually spend without guilt.

⏱ 8 min read 💎 Earn 25¢ 📈 Beginner 🎯 First-time budgeters

The 50/30/20 rule (your training wheels)

Forget complicated spreadsheets. The 50/30/20 rule is the most common starting framework — and it actually works. You take your after-tax income (what hits your account) and divide it three ways:

The split
50% needs · 30% wants · 20% savings + debt

Say your part-time campus job + freelance gigs net you $1,200/month. Here's how that breaks down:

  • $600 for needs — rent, groceries, your phone bill, gas, basic toiletries. Boring but non-negotiable.
  • $360 for wants — coffee runs, going out, the occasional Sephora basket, Spotify Premium, that concert ticket.
  • $240 for future-you — HYSA contributions, paying down debt, your Roth IRA (we'll get there).

How to actually do it this week

The framework is useless without a 20-minute action plan. Here's the move:

  1. Pull your last 30 days of transactions. Open your bank app, screenshot or export. No judgment yet — just data.
  2. Categorize quickly. Three buckets: needs, wants, savings/debt. Don't agonize — if it took less than 3 seconds to want, it's a want.
  3. Compare against 50/30/20. Where are you actually? If wants are at 50% and savings at 5%, you've found your leak.
  4. Pick ONE category to shift this month. Not all of them. Just one. Maybe wants drops from 45% → 35%. That's a win.
💡 The honest part

The 50/30/20 rule is a starting point, not a religion. If you live in a high-rent city, your needs might genuinely be 65% and that's fine — adjust wants and savings around it. The goal is awareness and intentionality, not perfection.

The common traps

Most first budgets fall apart in the same three places. Don't let yours:

  • Forgetting subscriptions. Spotify, Hulu, Netflix, gym, that meditation app, ChatGPT Plus — they add up to $80-150/month silently. Audit them monthly.
  • "Treat yourself" creep. A $7 oat latte three times a week is $84/month — which is $1,000/year. That's a flight to Europe. Not saying don't get the latte. Saying know what it costs.
  • Ignoring irregular costs. Birthdays, holidays, textbooks at the start of a semester. Build a small "annual stuff" line item (~$40/month) and you'll never be ambushed again.
⚡ Quick check

Your roommate takes home $1,000/month from her dorm job. Under the 50/30/20 rule, how much should she put toward savings + debt payoff?

✓ Exactly. 20% of $1,000 = $200. That's what the rule calls for, though she can always push higher if her needs are lower.
